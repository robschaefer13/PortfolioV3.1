Case study Hike Planning App

Problem statement

Design a mobile app for a client that enables the user the following abilities
(no other client direction/ideation provided):

  • Save content
  • Organize content
  • Create content
  • Upload files/content
  • Needs to be collaborative

My roles

Research, develop, structure, brand, and design application.

Solution

Design a simple, reliable and very informative user-driven (collaborative) mobile hike planning app that includes:

  • Hiking trail ratings & reviews
  • Shared user pictures of trails
  • Ability to make and save lists (for organizing)

Competitive Analysis

The hike planning app market is a surprisingly crowded one, with well over a dozen competitiors vying for market share. However, the top three were in a class by themselves.

Strengths

  • Fast searches
  • Trail info
  • User reviews/feedback
  • Simple maps
  • Offline map support
  • Trailhead finder

Nonetheless, I was surprised to find a lack of some very valuable features.

Weaknesses

  • Location of potable water and/or restroom
  • Parking availability
  • Trail difficulty ratings
  • Pet friendliness info
  • Current weather info
  • Current trail conditions
  • Safety alert feature/s

User Personas

Users want to know what features are available on which trails, and are frustrated without that data.

  • Jeanne

    Goals

    • Save time and energy with wise planning (and avoid possible “melt downs”)
    • Provide enhanced data for decision making (shade/tree canopy, scenic stops, insect issues)

    Concerns

    • Don’t want smartphone distracting from hike
    • Don’t want to spend much time learning about, downloading, and learning to use hiking app (or any app, for that matter)
    • Doesn’t want app draining too much battery (battery usually seems to be an issue before end of day)

  • Katie

    Goals

    • Plan group outings from online together
    • Would like help with safety precautions, and emergency trail exits
    • Know where restrooms are available

    Concerns

    • Planning hikes without knowing the quality of the trail, views, and time needed for hike for current fitness level
    • Lack of info for hiking/walking in less popular (lower traffic areas)
    • Doesn’t want app draining too much battery

Research

In 2018 approximately 93 million Americans will get outside to hike, backpack, mountain climb, and camp. Outdoor enthusiasts want to explore nature and experience life to the fullest, and they will spend nearly $290 billion in the pursuit.*

Potential users of our app are planning hikes with their phones, but the majority are not using a hike planning app to assist with that planning… the market is there.

Brand Creation

Top three adjectives to describe the brand:

  • Reliable
  • Informative
  • Collaborative

Brand Name: Step 1

The name is designed to invoke hiking “steps” as well as planning “steps”. In other words, the first step in a great hike is a great hike plan!

Taking the logo designing from sketching into software phase, I found myself intuitively moving towards a visual representation of hiking which was initially represented by a simplified hiker icon.

A major challenge learned during the process… what logos and icons render well small on a smartphone?

Site Map

The Site Map has four primary channels, which met all of the clients needs:

  1. Planning
  2. Saving Lists & Reviews
  3. User Account
  4. Help

The Planning channel (#1, above left) breaks into two channels: Research Trails vs. Browsing Trails. This issue wound up impacting my UX later in the process when I created a Home Screen and decided to represent this Plan Hike element with two different buttons, instead of one.

Conclusion

Again, despite being a crowded market, the research indicates that the need for better hike planning tools exists. If enough value-added options can be built into a new application, while retaining the strengths of the top competitors, the opportunity to quickly capture market share is ripe. Also, since the market for hiking and related outdoor gear is also strong, the app can be cross-branded and/or create major revenue streams from outdoor gear advertisers.

For example, a partnership with an established outdoor gear player (for example: Timberland hiking boots, Solomon trail shoes, or Suburaru sport utility vehicles) could help to leverage a dominance over the hike planning application field. As stated earlier, outdoor enthusiasts will spend nearly $290 billion in the pursuit of their interests.
